This weekend presents a visual delight for skywatchers.Each year around this time, the world gets to witness one of the best-known meteor showers, the Perseid meteor shower. While it was on August 13 and 14 last year, it would peak during late Sunday night, August 11, this year, into Monday early morning. During this time, there would almost be no moon, making the night sky ideal for spectators. Reportedly, the first quarter moon will set around 11:20 pm local daytime, a time when the sky is dark. Some of the Perseids are expected to appear in the evening. However, it is best viewed from about 11 pm or midnight until the early light next morning.

What is the Perseid meteor?

The name Perseid is believed to be derived from the Perseus constellation.It peaks during mid-August each year and is considered to be among the best meteor showers. Perseids, usually swift and bright, leave a trail of light and colour behind them as they traverse through the night sky. It is also one of the most plentiful showers with nearly 100 meteors seen per hour. This happens during warm summer night-time weather, offering skywatchers the most comfortable viewing experience.

Perseids are also widely popular for their fireballs or larger explosions of colour and light that occur longer than the usual meteor streaks as they come from larger particles composed of cometary materials. These fireballs are brighter and make for a distinct view

The debris that collides with our atmosphere leading to the creation of Perseids come from the Comet called 109/Swift-Tuttle. According to Nasa, this comet takes 133 years to orbit the Sun once. Late Italian astronomer Giovanni Schiaparelli is credited with finding the connection between comets and meteor showers. The comet Swift-Tuttle last visited the inner solar system in 1992. The comet, which was discovered in 1862 by astronomers Lewis Swift and Horace Tuttle, has a nucleus spanning 26 km in diametre.
